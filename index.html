Who We Are

A safe step forward, together

Silent Step was founded to provide Australians affected by domestic and family violence (DFV) with a trusted, easy-to-access hub of information, resources and connection to professional support services.

Domestic violence is not only physical. It includes emotional, psychological, financial, sexual and technology-facilitated abuse. It affects people of all genders, ages, cultures, and backgrounds.

Our mission is simple: no one should have to face this alone. We believe in your strength, your safety, and your right to live free from fear.

Recognise the Signs

Types of domestic & family violence

Abuse is not always physical. Understanding the different forms can help you recognise what is happening and take the next step.

🤕

Physical Abuse

  • Hitting, slapping, punching or kicking
  • Throwing objects or weapons
  • Strangulation or suffocation
  • Denying medical care
💔

Emotional & Psychological

  • Constant criticism, humiliation or name-calling
  • Controlling who you see or where you go
  • Threats to harm you, children or pets
  • Extreme jealousy and possessiveness
💸

Financial Abuse

  • Controlling all money and finances
  • Preventing you from working
  • Running up debts in your name
  • Demanding to see all spending
⚠️

Sexual Abuse

  • Forcing sexual activity against your will
  • Reproductive coercion
  • Sharing intimate images without consent
  • Treating you as a sexual object
📱

Technology-Facilitated

  • Monitoring your devices and accounts
  • Sharing private images or information
  • Using GPS or spy apps to track you
  • Online harassment and threats
🌏

Cultural & Spiritual

  • Using cultural or religious beliefs to control
  • Forced marriage
  • Isolating from cultural community
  • Denying right to practise faith

Remember: Abuse is never your fault. The responsibility always lies with the person causing harm. If any of these signs feel familiar, please reach out for support.

Protect Yourself

Safety Planning

A safety plan is a personalised, practical guide to help you protect yourself and your children. It can be adapted at any time.

If you're still at home

  • Identify safe rooms (avoid kitchens, bathrooms or garages where weapons may be present)
  • Memorise important numbers (1800RESPECT: 1800 737 732, a trusted friend, emergency: 000)
  • Alert neighbours, friends or family so they can call for help if needed
  • Know your exits and escape routes from your home
  • Keep a bag of essentials hidden or with a trusted person
  • Teach children a code word that means "go to a safe place"
  • Keep important documents in a safe place (passport, birth certificates, Medicare card)

Planning to leave

  • Plan the safest time and way to leave (when the abuser is out or asleep)
  • Have emergency cash, cards or valuables ready to take
  • Pack an emergency bag: ID, medication, phone charger, clothes, children's items
  • Contact a DV service to help with safe housing and support
  • Tell a trusted person your plan and check-in schedule
  • Take evidence of abuse if it's safe to do so (photos, medical records)
  • Have your car fuelled and parked for a quick exit

After leaving

  • Apply for an Apprehended Violence Order (AVO) / Domestic Violence Order (DVO)
  • Change locks and security codes at your new location
  • Inform your workplace, school and childcare of the situation
  • Update your address and contact details securely
  • Open a new bank account only you have access to
  • Connect with a local DV service for ongoing support and legal advice
  • Seek counselling and support for yourself and your children

Staying safe online

  • Use private/incognito browsing when researching domestic violence (Ctrl+Shift+N or ⌘+Shift+N)
  • Clear your browser history after visiting support websites
  • Change passwords using a device the abuser cannot access
  • Check devices for tracking or spy apps (ask a DV service for help)
  • Review location sharing on your phone and social media accounts
  • Create a new email account on a safe device for confidential communication
  • Be cautious about what you share on social media – location data can be tracked

For Friends & Family

How to help someone you care about

If you're concerned a friend, family member or colleague may be experiencing abuse, here's how you can help.

01

Listen without judgement

Create a safe space for them to talk. Believe what they tell you. Don't minimise their experience or pressure them to leave.

02

Ask gently

"I've noticed some things that worry me. Are you okay?" can open a door. Be patient – it may take time for them to open up.

03

Share information safely

Give them the 1800RESPECT number (1800 737 732) discreetly. Offer to help them access services when they're ready.

04

Support their choices

Leaving is complex and can be the most dangerous time. Respect their decisions and keep offering support – don't give ultimatums.

05

Look after yourself too

Supporting someone through DFV can be emotionally exhausting. Reach out to 1800RESPECT yourself for guidance and support.

06

Know the warning signs

Withdrawal from friends and family, unexplained injuries, partner controlling finances or movements, fear of partner's reactions.
